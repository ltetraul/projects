The Game Within The Game: Visualizing Off-Ball Movement Analytics in Professional Basketball

Lucas Tetrault
5 min read
·
Mar 4, 2026

When watching basketball, our eyes tend to naturally follow the ball. We notice things like the crossover, the three pointer or the slam dunk. The stats we look at after the game like points, assists, turnovers, etc. are important but do not tell the whole story. Four offensive players are almost always moving without the ball, setting screens, cutting and shifting defenders around in ways that completely shape the outcome of the play. The paper “OBTracker: Visual Analytics of Off-ball Movements in Basketball” published in IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics focuses on that hidden side of the game that is often overlooked. Using tracking data from the NBA, the authors built a system that tries to measure and visualize how valuable off-ball movement really is to teams.

The main question that they are asking is simple but important. How do we measure the impact of players who do not have the ball in their hands? This matters because so much of basketball is about timing and creating space. A perfectly timed cut can force a defender to help, which leads to an open shooter somewhere else. The cutter might not even touch the ball, but they still played a part in creating an advantage or mismatch. Traditional stats just simply do not capture things like that. The researchers also identified three main challenges: How to gain meaningful insights from massive tracking datasets, how to group similar movement patterns while still accounting for context like time and score and how to explain these movements as dynamic processes instead of just frozen moments.

In order to solve this, they built a visual analytics system called OBTracker. They started with 631 NBA games with over 130 million frames of player tracking data. They identified who had the ball, which players were cutting and who was setting the screens. They filtered out slow or irrelevant movements so that they were only analyzing real off-ball actions. Once the data was cleaned up and ready to go, they built a model to estimate how much each off-ball movement improved a team’s chances of scoring.

One thing I liked is that they did not just throw a deep learning model at the problem and call it a day. Instead, they focused on interpretability. They defined a movement’s contribution as the change in scoring expectation from the start of the cut to the end. Scoring expectation is calculated by multiplying shooting expectation by passing probability. Shooting expectation looks at how much space a player controls using spatial modeling and how well defenders are positioned along with how good the player historically shoots from that area of the court. Passing probability is estimated using a regression model that considers passing distance and defensive pressure along the passing lane. It is not perfect but it makes sense which is a big deal if you actually want coaches to trust it.

The visualization side is where things get pretty interesting. OBTracker lets users analyze off-ball movement at three levels. At the team level, you can see the most common cutting patterns and how effective they are. Instead of dividing the court into random grids they use functional regions that make more sense in basketball terms. At the player level, you can compare which player combinations execute certain movements best by ranking them by things like shooting expectation or passing probability. Action level allows you to watch how a specific off-ball movement unfolds over time and see how space opens up or how defenders react. It basically turns something mundane like studying film into something more interactive.

When testing their scoring expectation metric, they found that possessions with higher predicted scoring expectations tend to result in more points. The relationship was not very major since basketball is chaotic and unpredictable but it was there. They were also honest about the limitations. The regression model and screen detection systems have their flaws and they do not fully incorporate movement speed or direction. There is definitely some room to improve but the foundation is still very strong.

What stood out the most is how practical this feels. A lot of sports analytics research is either super theoretical or kind of a black-box but this feels like something that could actually live in a coaching environment. It can actually help coaches figure out which players execute their plays the best and why a specific play works or fails. It is structured in a way that makes sense from a basketball perspective instead of just to data scientists even though some of the visualizations can definitely take a while to understand. I think the next step would be visualizing their findings in a simpler way that anyone is able to look at and understand quickly by utilizing some of the design tactics we have learned in our data visualization class.

Ultimately, OBTracker does a great job shifting the spotlight to the invisible magic that is happening away from the ball. It reminds us that offense is not only about who scores but also who is making the right moves at the right time. The next time you are watching a game, try ignoring the ball for a possession or two and instead focus on all of the weak-side action that is happening. This hidden layer in the game is crucial to team success and is a lot more important than most box scores or statistics give credit for.

Further Questions

Could systems like OBTracker shift how players are valued in contracts or scouting? What are the ethical implications of increasingly detailed player tracking data? Should we be quantifying “invisible” contributions like this? How transferable do you think this approach would be to other team sports like soccer or hockey?

Work Cited

Wu, Yihong, et al. “OBTracker: Visual Analytics of Off-Ball Movements in Basketball.” IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics, 27 Sept. 2022, https://doi.org/10.1109/tvcg.2022.3209373.
